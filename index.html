About Our Visualisation

Standard of living refers to the quantity and quality of material goods and services available to a given population. It represents the material abundance of people in the area in which they live and is also one of the most important determinants of happiness or well-being (Boyle, 2021; Grossnationalhappiness.com, 2015). Improving living standards has long been an important goal in national plans in both developed and developing countries (RRHBA, 2021). Moreover, various disciplines and institutions have widely studied the assessment of living standards (Fontinelle, 2021).

As early as 1978, the United Nations published the System of Social and Demographic Statistics (SSDS), which proposed 12 categories to assess living standards (United Nations Statistics Division, 2011). The HDI is a score that shows a country's standard of living and development potential. However, although the HDI is a simplified and accepted limited assessment of human development, it has also been subject to many criticisms. Many economists argue that it is inadequate and redundant, as the indicators of the HDI are highly correlated with the per capita income indicator and that it is simpler to use national per capita income (Amadeo, 2021). Moreover, the project only presents differences at the national level, not at the city level, and not at the regional level. Inspired by these shortcomings, we have chosen to use four categories of the SSDS indicators to show regional and temporal differences and variations in living standards in different aspects.

Research Questions

  • How have the Living standards in London changed over space and time?


    The visualisation objectives in the project focus on three key areas. To provide a platform for demonstrating changes in living standards. Observation of more minor levels (urban level and regional level) Viewing living standards through different facets (4 indicator categories).

Future Improvements

  • Uniformity of style and pattern

    a unified JS module and style is necessary and inevitable in trying to add more categories of indicators. Secondly, with a uniform style, the different indicator visualisations can be differentiated by using different colour families. Furthermore, to give the user a more interactive experience and uncover more information, it is possible to construct a function that allows several indicator visualisations to be compared on the same page. By sliding the Time slider, the user can compare several indicators in a specific area over time.

  • Additional Total Score Judging

    Although our visualisation aims to show changes in various aspects of living standards, it is also essential to add a total score, which gives the user a conclusive message that, when combined with the various aspects of the visualisation shown previously, is believed to give the user more food for thought and inspiration.
